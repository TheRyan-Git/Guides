# 🚣 Rowing Guide

Rowing is wonderfully demanding and complex. It requires full-body strength, cardiovascular endurance, precise technique, and exceptional teamwork. Whether you're a complete beginner or ready for the next level, this guide will help you.

## Types of Rowing

### By Equipment Type

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Sculling** | Each rower holds **two oars** (one in each hand). |
| **Sweep Rowing** | Each rower holds **one oar** with both hands. Boats require even numbers of rowers. |
| **Ergometer (Erg)** | Indoor rowing machine (Concept2 is the standard). Used for training and testing. |

### By Boat Type
- **Sculling boats:** 1x (single), 2x (double), 4x (quad)
- **Sweep boats:** 2- (pair), 4- (four), 4+ (four with coxswain), 8+ (eight with coxswain)
**Note:** The + means there is a coxswain

## The Rowing Stroke

The rowing stroke has **four phases**, executed smoothly and continuously:

```
CATCH DRIVE FINISH RECOVERY
```

### 1. The Catch
- Knees fully compressed, shins **vertical**
- Body leaned forward ~20° past vertical (from hips, not waist)
- Arms extended, blades squared and hovering above the water
- Ready to drop the blade in and apply power

> Common mistake: pulling with arms before legs are extended

### 2. The Drive
The power phase — executed in strict order:
1. **Legs push** — press the footboard away with your legs
2. **Body swing** — once legs are ~75% extended, swing your body back through vertical to ~20° past vertical
3. **Arms draw** — pull the handle to the lower chest/abdomen

> Common mistake: using arms and back too early.

### 3. The Finish
- Legs flat, body leaned back ~20°
- Hands drawn to the lower chest/abdomen
- Elbows pointed slightly back and outside the body
- Blade fully submerged and at 90° to the boat

> Common mistake: finishing too early

### 4. The Recovery
The controlled return to the catch — executed in **reverse order** of the drive:
1. Arms extend first
2. Body rocks forward
3. Knees compress (slide forward)

> Common mistake: not actively setting the boat (with tilt, and oar height (more info later))

**The recovery should take roughly twice as long as the drive**, your muscles should be relaxed in the recovery.

## Boat Classes & Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Coxswain (Cox)** | The person who steers and commands the crew; does not row |
| **Stroke seat** | Rower closest to the stern; sets the rhythm for the crew |
| **Bow seat** | Rower closest to the bow (front of the boat) |
| **Split** | Your average time to row 500 meters (shown as 2:00/500m, for example) |
| **Rating (SPM)** | Strokes per minute — how many strokes the boat takes each minute |
| **Drive ratio** | Ratio of drive time to recovery time (ideally ~1:2 or 1:2.5) |
| **Feathering** | Rotating the oar blade from vertical (squared) to horizontal during recovery |
| **Catching a crab** | When a blade gets stuck in the water, and the rower loses control of the oar |
| **Set** | The balance of the boat from side to side |

## Technique Breakdown

### Common Coaching Cues
- **"Legs-body-arms, arms-body-legs"** — the sequence of the drive and recovery
- **"Don't shoot the slide"** — don't push with your legs and let your body get pulled forward.
- **"Sit tall"** — avoid hunching; maintain a long, relaxed spine
- **"Quiet hands at the catch"** — avoid splashing; clean blade entry matters

### Key Body Positions

| Phase | Spine | Shoulders | Arms |
|-------|-------|-----------|------|
| Catch | Tall, forward lean | Relaxed, not hunched | Fully extended |
| Drive | Progressively upright | Connected to the back swing | Draw late |
| Finish | Leaned back ~20° | Drawn back, relaxed | Pulled to abdomen |
| Recovery | Rocks forward | Relaxed | Fully extend first |

## Training Principles

Rowing training is divided into different **intensity zones**:

| Zone | Name | % Max HR | RPE (1–10) | Purpose |
|------|------|----------|------------|---------|
| 1 | Recovery | <60% | 1–3 | Active recovery, rebuilding |
| 2 | Aerobic Base | 60–70% | 4–5 | Building endurance (bulk of training) |
| 3 | Aerobic Threshold | 70–80% | 6–7 | Race pace conditioning |
| 4 | Lactate Threshold | 80–90% | 7–8 | Speed at high effort |
| 5 | Anaerobic / VO₂ Max | 90–100% | 9–10 | All-out intervals |

### Training Distribution
For most competitive rowers:
- **~80% of total volume** should be in Zone 1–2 (low intensity, long duration)
- **~20%** in Zone 3–5 (higher intensity work)

This is called **polarized training** and is the most supported approach for endurance athletes.

## Common Mistakes

- **Breaking the sequence** — using arms before the legs are finished
- **Rushing the slide** — shooting the seat back too fast during recovery
- **Hunching the back** at the catch — leads to injury and power loss
- **Overtraining** — more volume without recovery leads to regression and injury
- **Ignoring erg work** — water time alone isn't enough; erg training is essential
- **Catching crabs** due to blade timing issues — stay relaxed at the catch
- **Going out too hard** in a race — the middle 1000m is where races are won or lost
