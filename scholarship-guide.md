## What Is a Scholarship?

A **scholarship** is **free money** awarded to students based on merit, need, identity, talent, or community affiliation. They often write a check, and trust that you will follow through.

---

## Types of Scholarships

| Type | Based On | Examples |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Merit-based** | GPA, test scores, achievements | National Merit, AP Scholar |
| **Need-based** | Financial circumstances | Pell Grant, FAFSA-linked awards |
| **Identity-based** | Background, ethnicity, gender | Gates Millennium, UNCF |
| **Talent-based** | Arts, athletics, STEM | Regeneron STS, music competitions |
| **Local/Community** | Where you live or work | Rotary Club, local businesses |
| **Essay contests** | Writing quality | Ayn Rand Institute, Scholastic |

## Where to Find Scholarships

### Free Search Engines
- **[Fastweb](https://www.fastweb.com)**: large database, personalizable
- **[Scholarships.com](https://www.scholarships.com)**: broad search tool
- **[College Board Scholarship Search](https://bigfuture.collegeboard.org/scholarship-search)**: trusted source
- **[Bold.org](https://bold.org)**: fast-growing, modern platform

### School & Community Sources
- Your high school's **guidance counselor** office
- Your **college's financial aid** office
- **Employers** (your parents' workplaces often offer awards)
- **Local civic organizations**: Rotary Club, Lions Club, Knights of Columbus
- **Religious institutions** and affiliated foundations
- **State scholarship programs** (search "[your state] scholarship")

### Niche Scholarships
Don't overlook unusual awards; scholarships exist for left-handed students, tall people, people named "Murphy," and hundreds of other quirky qualifications. Use search terms like _"scholarship for [your hobby/interest/background]"_.

## How to Write a Winning Essay

Most scholarship essays ask one of three core questions:

1. **Tell us about yourself / your background.**
2. **What are your goals and how will this scholarship help?**
3. **Describe a challenge you overcame.**

### Tips for a Strong Essay

- **Start with a hook:** a vivid story, recall a scene.
- **Be specific**
- **Show, don't tell.** Describe moments that reveal your character, instead of saying "I am great".
- **Answer the actual prompt.** Re-read the prompt after drafting to ensure you addressed it directly (hint: they likely have a rubric that you can figure out from the prompt or website).
- **End with a clear connection** between your goals and why this scholarship matters. Name-drop if possible (but do it well).
- **Proofread.** Typos will destroy you.

### Essay Length
Most prompts ask for 250–650 words. Shorter prompts are 100–250 words.

## Letters of Recommendation

### Who to Ask
- Teachers who know you well and recently
- Coaches, mentors, or supervisors who can speak to your character and work ethic
- Avoid asking parents or relatives

### How to Ask
1. Ask **at least 4–6 weeks** before the deadline.
2. Provide the recommender with:
   - The scholarship name and deadline
   - A copy of your resume or activity list
   - Your personal statement or essay draft (if available)
   - Submission instructions (email, portal link, etc.)
3. Follow up one week before the deadline with a polite reminder.
4. Always send a **thank-you note** afterward.

## Staying Organized

Managing multiple applications requires a system. Use a spreadsheet to track:

| Column | What to Track |
|--------|---------------|
| Scholarship Name | Full title of the award |
| Amount | Dollar value |
| Deadline | Application due date |
| Requirements | Essays, transcripts, recs needed |
| Status | Not started / In progress / Submitted / Won |
| Notes | Portal link, login info |

Set calendar reminders **2 weeks** and **3 days** before each deadline.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Missing deadlines**: set alerts early
- **Generic essays** that could apply to any scholarship
- **Ignoring small scholarships**: $500 awards add up fast
- **Not following instructions** (wrong word count, missing documents)
- **Applying to only big national scholarships**: local awards have far better odds
- **Waiting until senior year**: many awards are OPEN to 9th–11th graders
